# Dialogues

## First Meeting (Very Easy)

```sentence-n
アリス：はじめまして。お 名前 (なまえ)は？
Alice: It is the first time (we meet). What’s your name?
```

```sentence-n
マサル：僕 (ぼく)はマサルと 言 (い)います。あなたは？
Masaru: I’m Masaru. And you?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：私 (わたし)はアリスです。[1] はじめまして。
Alice: I’m Alice. Nice to meet you.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：アリスさんは 学生 (がくせい)ですか？
Masaru: Alice, are you a student?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：はい、私 (わたし)は 語学学校 (ごがくがっこう)で 勉強 (べんきょう)しています。マサルさんは？
Alice: Yes, I’m studying at a language school. What about you, Masaru?
```

```sentence-n
マサル：僕 (ぼく)は 働 (はたら)いています。お 国 (くに)はどちらですか？
Masaru: I’m working. Which country are you from?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：私 (わたし)はイギリスから 来 (き)ました。
Alice: I’m from the UK.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：へえ、ロンドン 出身 (しゅっしん)ですか？
Masaru: Oh, are you from London?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：いえ、ロンドンじゃなくて、バーミンガムです。
Alice: No, not London, Birmingham.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：そうなんですか。いつ 日本 (にほん)に 来 (き)ましたか？
Masaru: I see. When did you come to Japan?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：去年 (きょねん) 来 (き)ました。
Alice: I came last year.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：へえ。あのー、連絡先 (れんらくさき) 交換 (こうかん)できませんか？
Masaru: I see. Um… could we exchange contact information?
```

```sentence-n
アリス：いいですよ。私 (わたし)のLINEのQRコードを 出 (だ)しますね。
Alice: Sure. I’ll show you my LINE QR code.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：スキャンしました。今度 (こんど)いっしょに 食事 (しょくじ)でも 行 (い)きましょう。
Masaru: I scanned it. Let’s go have some meal together sometime.
```

```sentence-n
アリス：いいですね。私 (わたし)の 彼氏 (かれし)、日本人 (にほんじん)の 友達 (ともだち)を 欲 (ほ)しがっていたので、[2] よかったです。[3]
Alice: Sounds good. My boyfriend has been wanting Japanese friends, so this is great.
```

```sentence-n
マサル：そうなんですか。心 (こころ)の 声 (こえ)：彼氏 (かれし)がいるのか。チクショー！
Masaru: I see… (inner voice: She has a boyfriend? Damn it!)
```

### Notas

1. When introducing your own name, you can use either the AはBです or AはBと 言 (い)います
   structure. AはBです means “A is B”, as in “I’m Masaru”. AはBと 言 (い)います means “I am called
   Masaru (My name is Masaru).” AはBです sounds more casual, while AはBと 言 (い)います is more
   formal. You can also say AはBと 申 (もう)します for even greater formality.

1. 〜がっている is a tricky grammar point. If you are a beginner, please skip this explanation
   and just learn the phrase as it is. This grammar is used to describe someone’s
   outward behavior that expresses their inner feelings. 〜がっている is the teiru-form of
   〜がる, which basically turns an i-adjective into a verb. For example, if you add がる to
   痛 (いた)い (hurts), it will become 痛 (いた)がる, which refers to the act of expressing the
   inner feeling of pain. While playing soccer, a ball struck the boy in the groin, and
   he doubled over in agony, clutching himself and crying out, “Ahhh!” This outward
   display of pain is the act of 痛 (いた)がる.

1. In Japanese, directly stating someone else’s inner feelings in an assertive way is
   unnatural, since you are not supposed to know exactly how another person feels. In
   English, it sounds natural to say “my boyfriend wants Japanese friends”, but in
   Japanese, saying 彼氏 (かれし)は 日本人 (にほんじん)の 友達 (ともだち)を 欲 (ほ)しいです is unnatural, because
   “I” cannot directly express what “my boyfriend” feels inside. Instead, when your
   boyfriend outwardly expresses the wish “I want to have Japanese friends”, you would
   use 〜がる to describe this act of expressing his feelings: 彼氏 (かれし)は 日本人 (にほんじん)の 友達
   (ともだち)を 欲 (ほ)しがっています. 3. よかったです is the past tense (polite form) of the adjective 良
   (よ)い, which literally means “good”, so よかったです literally means “it was good”.

   In Japanese, this expression is used when something turns out to be good. For
   example, imagine you are worried about something, say, someone you know was missing
   in the woods, and you were concerned about him. But then it turns out that he came
   back safely. In this situation, since things turned out fine, you would say よかったです
   (It was good that things turned out well). In the dialogue, Alice says よかったです because
   things worked out in her favor: her boyfriend might gain the Japanese friend he
   wanted. It’s like saying, “I’m glad things turned out this way”.

## Vocabulary

- はじめまして： “It is the first time (we meet)” (A common greeting phrase when meeting
  someone for the first time.)
- お 名前 (なまえ)は？: “What’s your name?” (お 名前 (なまえ)は？ is an incomplete sentence because it
  has no predicate. The full form is お 名前 (なまえ)は 何 (なん)ですか？, but it’s very common to
  omit the ending.)
- 僕 (ぼく): “I” (a first-person pronoun typically used by men)
- AはBと 言 (い)います: “A is called B.” / “My name is B.”
- あなたは？: “And you?” / “How about you?”
- 私 (わたし): I (the most common first-person pronoun used by both men and women)
- AはBです: “A is B”
- 学生 (がくせい): student
- 〜ですか？: a sentence ending used to ask a question
- はい: yes
- 語学学校 (ごがくがっこう): language school
- 〜で 勉強 (べんきょう)しています: “(I’m) studying at ~”
- 働 (はたら)いています: “(I’m) working”
- お 国 (くに)はどちらですか？: “Which country are you from?”
- AはBから 来 (き)ました: “A came from B”
- イギリス: the UK
- へえ: an interjection used when learning something (like “I see”)
- ロンドン: London
- 〜出身 (しゅっしん): “from ~” / “originating from ~” (used for where someone comes from)
- いえ: no
- Aじゃなくて、B: “Not A, but B”
- バーミンガム: Birmingham
- そうなんですか: an interjection used when learning something (similar to へえ, “I see”)
- いつ: when
- 日本 (にほん): Japan
- 来 (き)ました: came
- か？: question particle
- 去年 (きょねん): last year
- あのー: a filler word often used before a question or request (like “Um…” or “Well…”)
- 連絡先 (れんらくさき) 交換 (こうかん)できませんか？: “Can we exchange contact information?”
- いいですよ: “Sure” / “That’s fine”
- LINE: the most common messaging app in Japan
- QRコード: QR code
- 〜を 出 (だ)します: “I’ll show you ~”
- スキャンしました: “I scanned it”
- 今度 (こんど): “next time”
- いっしょに: together
- 食事 (しょくじ)しましょう: “Let’s have a meal”
- いいですね: “Sounds good” / “That’s nice”
- 彼氏 (かれし): boyfriend
- 日本人 (にほんじん): Japanese person/people
- 友達 (ともだち): friend
- 〜を 欲 (ほ)しがっていた: “(He) wanted (to have) ~”
- 〜ので: because (used to explain a reason)
- よかったです: “That’s great” / “I’m glad”
- 心 (こころ)の 声 (こえ): inner voice
- 彼氏 (かれし)がいるのか: “So, (you) have a boyfriend...” (a realization)
- チクショー: literally “beast/animal,” but used as an exclamation of anger or frustration
  when something doesn’t go one’s way. Roughly, “Damn it!”
